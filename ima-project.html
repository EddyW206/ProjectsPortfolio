Improving the Student Experience at the UW IMA Facility

A UX research project to uncover pain points and identify opportunities for enhancing the university's primary fitness center.

Role

Sole UX Researcher

Timeline

4 Weeks (Academic Project)

Methods

Field Study, User Interviews, Surveys

1. The Problem

The University of Washington's Intramural Activities (IMA) building is a vital hub for student wellness, serving thousands daily. While popular, anecdotal evidence suggested that many students, especially newcomers, found the facility overwhelming and difficult to navigate.

My goal was to move beyond anecdotes and systematically investigate the student experience, asking: "What are the primary pain points students encounter when using the IMA, and how can the experience be improved?"

2. My Approach

To get a holistic view, I employed a mixed-methods approach to first understand user behavior in context, then dive deeper into their motivations and finally quantify the issues at scale.

Observational Field Study

I conducted a 3-hour observational study during peak hours to understand how students naturally interacted with the space, noting common paths, points of confusion, and workarounds.

In-Depth Interviews

I conducted four semi-structured interviews with a mix of frequent and infrequent IMA users to explore their goals, frustrations, and feelings about the facility.

Survey

Finally, I designed and distributed a survey (n=19) to validate the qualitative findings and measure the prevalence of the identified pain points across a wider student sample.

3. What I Discovered

After synthesizing the data, three major themes emerged as key areas of friction in the student experience.

Insight 1: "Analysis Paralysis" in the Weight Room

Many students, particularly those newer to fitness, felt intimidated by the crowded and complex layout of the free-weight and machine areas. This led to them either sticking to familiar cardio machines or leaving without completing their intended workout.

"I just walk in, see how packed it is, and I don't know where anything is. It's easier to just go run on the treadmill." — Interview Participant, 2nd Year

Insight 2: The Search for Equipment is a Workout Itself

Survey data confirmed a major frustration: finding available equipment. Students spent significant time wandering, looking for open machines or specific items like foam rollers or certain dumbbell weights.

Time Spent Looking for Equipment (per visit)

Insight 3: Underutilized Spaces and Services

Interviews and survey free-responses revealed that many students were unaware of less-crowded spaces like the 3rd-floor mat area or services like the free equipment orientations. This information was not easily discoverable.

4. Recommendations

Based on the research, I proposed three actionable recommendations to address the key pain points and improve the overall student experience.

  1. Digital Navigation & Zone Map: Create a mobile-friendly interactive map on the IMA website. This map would show real-time crowd levels for different zones and allow users to locate specific machines or equipment types, reducing search time and anxiety.
  2. Improved Physical Wayfinding: Install clear, color-coded signage throughout the facility. "Beginner-Friendly Zones" could be clearly marked to help new users feel more comfortable and confident exploring different workout areas.
  3. Promotional Campaign for Underused Services: Launch a targeted awareness campaign at the start of each quarter via social media and in-facility digital screens to highlight services like equipment orientation and feature less-crowded workout spaces.

Reflection

This project was a fantastic exercise in applying sociological research methods to a real-world user experience problem. It reinforced the importance of using a mixed-methods approach to not only identify *what* the problems are but *why* they are happening. If I were to do this again with more resources, I would love to conduct a diary study to track student experiences over a longer period.
